Project: Air Pollution and Health

This project will investigate various data on air pollution and health, ultimately aiming to discover relationships and uncover any correlations between the two.

  1. Pollution levels 1990-2018 by country and gas type. This is to get an idea of countries at the top and bottom of the rankings. data: climatewatch
  2. Deaths attributable to the environment (%) and Air Pollution Exposure by country (pm2.5) ambient pollution attributable deaths/100,000 data: WHO. OECD data for mean population exposure to pm2.5
  3. Total historical / current output of pollution and current life expectancy by country. America is now very developed but has polluted the most over time.
  4. C02 emissions per capita and doctors per 1,000 people. Are currently highly polluting countries less developed?
  5. Pollution and happiness by country (over time or a bar chart for a year)
  6. Recent pollution and economic growth rates - do countries currently polluting heavily grow quickly? Can we expect their health to increase in the future like for the UK and US?

Item 2: Regressing Pollution Exposure on Pollution-attributable deaths (2019)

The low R squared value in this visualisation signifies a very weak positive correlation between pollution exposure and pollution-attributable deaths in a population. In some sense, I don't think this is surprising, as given the complexities and multitide of entire countries socioeconomically and developmentally, I would expect many other variables to be at play. Two examples within the data and my qualitative assessments: Niger has one of the highest exposures but lowest attributable-death rates. This doesn't mean pollution doesn't cause death here, but if we think about Niger, it is highly under-developed, with only around 50% of the population having access to clean water. As such, out of every 1,000,000 inhabitants, there is likely to be many other variables causing death. Secondly, there is undoubtedly a cluster of Central and Eastern European nations with farily low exposure levels but high attributable-death rates. This may compliment the Niger observation - take Ukraine - less developed than Finland which has low exposure and death rates, but far more developed than Niger, and so deaths from common illnesses (e.g. flu) are seldom, thus opening space for environment-related deaths, for which healthcare isn't great enough to subside.

Additionally, exposure levels look around 20-60% higher in Eastern compared to Western Europe. Comparing to Belgium for example, deaths are far lower although exposure similar. This result reflects possible wider differences between the countries, such as healthcare and nutrition, better states of which may explain lower attributable death rates. The existence of countless other variables means we are pushed to understand the bigger picture within which pollution and health exist, which is attmpted in my next visualisation.
