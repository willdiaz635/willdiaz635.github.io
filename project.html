Item 2: Regressing Pollution Exposure on Pollution-attributable deaths (2019)

The low R squared value in this visualisation signifies a positive but basically insignificant positive relationship between pollution exposure and pollution-attributable deaths in a population. In some sense, I don't think this is surprising, as given the complexities and multitide of entire countries socioeconomically and developmentally, I would expect many other variables to be at play. Two examples within the data and my qualitative assessments: Niger has one of the highest exposures but lowest attributable-death rates. This doesn't mean pollution doesn't cause death here, but if we think about Niger, it is highly under-developed, with only around 50% of the population having access to clean water. As such, out of every 1,000,000 inhabitants, there is likely to be many other variables causing death. Secondly, there is undoubtedly a cluster of Central and Eastern European nations with farily low exposure levels but high attributable-death rates. This may compliment the Niger observation - take Ukraine - less developed than Finland which has low exposure and death rates, but far more developed than Niger, and so deaths from common illnesses (e.g. flu) are seldom, thus opening space for environment-related deaths, for which healthcare isn't great enough to subside.

Additionally, exposure levels look around 20-60% higher in Eastern compared to Western Europe. Comparing to Belgium for example, deaths are far lower although exposure similar. This result reflects possible wider differences between the countries, such as healthcare and nutrition, better states of which may explain lower attributable death rates. The existence of countless other variables means we are pushed to understand the bigger picture within which pollution and health exist, which is attmpted in my next visualisation.

Item 3: Greenhouse Gas Emissions | HDI | GDP per Capita

To try and understand the relationship between pollution and health, one needs to look at more aspects shaping countries. In the visualisation below, it is striking that those countries polluting more per capita have higher HDI values. HDI is very closely correlated with health, especially given health is a component of HDI. This changes the perspective on pollution and health: today's rich countries are very healthy, and often to get there they have polluted a large amount, and most likely still are.

This isn't to say pollution is good for you. Of course fine particulate Greenhouse Gas matter one would expect to be bad for you. This motivates one to look at more focused data sets, in the belief there must be evidence, given fewer confounding variables, that pollution is bad for you.
