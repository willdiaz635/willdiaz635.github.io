Project: Pollution, Health, and Wealth

Aims: This project aims to uncover significant correlations, or interesting relationships, between countries through their states of pollution, health, and wealth. I think the way pollution links to different levels of health and wealth will be different across the world. Many countries have accumulated wealth through huge pollution, such as the UK. However some of the richest nations with the longest life expectancies today are historically environmentally-friendly, such as Finland. Additionally, some very poor countries have low pollution and bad health, or high pollution and bad health. There are non-uniform connections between these three major topics, which makes them interesting to collectively investigate. (103 words)

Item 1: Different Countries and their Greenhouse Gas emissions: high and low

This chart looks at various countries' emissions of Greenhouse Gases as categorised into six groups in the Kyoto Protocol. I have chosen a selction of countries which vary economically, in terms of their Human Development Indexes, and in terms of health. As such, this visualisation represents a platform from which further analysis shall take place, but will mainly serve to understand why countries have certain health and wealth statuses in relation to their pollution levels. A key observation is that absolute economic output is certainly linked to pollution - with the US and China by far the two largest economies in the world and the biggest historical polluters. This is similar for India and Russia for example. However, all four have very different health profiles and income levels per household. (142 words)

Item 2: Regressing Pollution Exposure on Pollution-attributable deaths (2019)

The world wants to understand more than ever how pollution affects our planet, as well as human beings. It may seem logical that in countries with loads of pollution per area unit, there are higher pollution-related death rates. However, as shown in the regression below, there is a basically insignificant positive correlation, with a 0.02 R squared value. A lot of the countries with high pollution exposure are notably poor African and Asian ones, yet many have low environment-attributable deaths. This may be because, for every 1000 deaths, given the countries are so poor, there are many other sources of death like malnutrition which diminish the figure. (107 words)

Item 3: Greenhouse Gas Emissions and GDP per Capita

To try and understand the relationship between pollution and health, one needs to look at more aspects shaping countries. In the visualisation below, it is striking that those countries polluting more per capita have higher HDI values. HDI is very closely correlated with health, especially given health is a component of HDI. This changes the perspective on pollution and health: today's rich countries are very healthy, and often to get there they have polluted a large amount, and most likely still are. (91 words)

Item 4: Historical Pollution and Life Expectancy

Shown below is a very weak, insignificant correlation between total historical Carbon Dioxide Emissions and Life Expectancy. I think this may signifiy a few things. Firstly, it is not necessarily the case that countries which pollute a lot can get rich and then get healthy. It is intersting to think about: pollution itself in an immediate sense, absent of good health and quality of life everyone knows is bad for you. This seems to be reflected in Sub-Saharan African nations for example. However, the US and Germany have high life expectancies, with their wealth and subsequent good health systems subsiding negative health impacts from health pollution in the long run. (117 words)
